Part one

What this is, and why it's worth twenty minutes

Claude, in plain language

Claude is an AI assistant you talk to in plain language — and you can write to it in Dutch just as easily as English; it understands both. You type what you need (a question, a document to summarise, an email to draft) and it responds. There's no special language to learn and no buttons to memorise. If you can write a message to a colleague, you can use Claude.

How this differs from the free chatbots you may have seen

This is the same underlying Claude, but running inside the firm's own secure, paid workspace rather than a free public website. That matters in three ways:

  • It can work with your actual files and tools — your documents, our SharePoint, and (once installed) Excel and Word — not just a blank chat box.
  • The firm's data is protected here in a way it is not on a free consumer tool (more in Part 7).
  • You have a proper account and the firm has oversight, which is what makes it appropriate for real work.

What it's genuinely good at

Drafting and rewriting; summarising long or dense documents; extracting structured information from material you give it (a table of key terms from a stack of leases, say); answering questions about documents you provide; translating; brainstorming; and explaining things you don't understand. For a document-heavy business like ours, that's a large share of the working day.

Where to stay sharp

Claude can occasionally state something wrong with complete confidence — this is the single most important thing to understand. Double-check numbers, dates, citations, and specific legal or regulatory points; a misread rent figure looks exactly as confident as a correct one. So the firm-wide rule is simple: by default, a human reviews anything that affects a decision or leaves the building. Claude does the heavy lifting and gives you a strong first draft; the judgement stays with you.

It feels slower before it feels faster — that's normal

For the first few days, working with Claude can feel clumsy. You're learning how to ask, and it doesn't yet know you. This early dip is completely normal — everyone goes through it. Push past the first week and it starts to genuinely save you time. Don't judge the tool by day one.

Your account: a Teams seat under the firm's Enterprise

  • Your chats and files are private to you by default. Colleagues cannot see your individual conversations.
  • Under our Enterprise terms, our data is not used to train Anthropic's models by default, and how long data is kept is set at the workspace level under the firm's plan.
  • Some things are shared by design — a Project you create can be shared with colleagues, and overall usage is visible at the firm level for governance.

The upshot: it's safe to do real work here in a way it would not be on a free public chatbot — as long as you follow the data rules in Part 7.

Part two

Opening the app: signing in and finding your way

Signing in

You already have the Claude app on your computer. Open it and sign in with your Rubens Capital Partners work email — this puts you on the firm's secure workspace, where the data protections in Part 7 apply. If sign-in doesn't work first time, that's almost always an account setup detail rather than anything you did. (If you don't have a Claude account yet, ask Simone.)

A quick tour of the window

The first time you open it, you'll mostly see a big box that says something like "Message Claude." Here's where the few things you'll actually use are:

The Claude desktop app. You only need six things to start.
  • 1New chat. Start a fresh conversation. Use one per topic.
  • 2Chat / Cowork / Code. Switch between the ways of working (Part 3).
  • 3Settings. Your preferences, and Settings → Usage to see your limits.
  • 4Attach a file. Add a PDF, Word doc, or spreadsheet to the chat.
  • 5Model & effort. Click the model name to switch model (Part 4).
  • 6Send. Send your message (or just press Enter).
Two things people always ask

How do I add a document? Click the paperclip (4), or simply drag the file onto the chat. You can also paste text straight in.

How do I get the answer out? Select Claude's reply and copy it into your email or Word document. For bigger pieces of work, Cowork can save a finished file for you directly (Part 3).

Part three

The ways to work with Claude

There isn't just one "Claude." There are a few different surfaces, each suited to a different kind of work. The quick version:

If you want to…Use
Ask a question, draft an email, or summarise a document you paste or uploadChat
Have Claude work through a multi-step task across several files and hand you finished outputCowork
Update an Excel model or draft in Word without leaving the appClaude in Microsoft 365
Write code or do heavy technical / data workClaude Code (optional)

Chat — your everyday on-ramp

Where most people start and spend most of their time. Open a chat, type what you need, optionally attach a file. Ask it to draft, summarise, rewrite, explain, or compare. If you only ever use Chat, you'll already get enormous value. Two things worth knowing:

  • Projects group related chats with reference files and standing instructions, so Claude has the background every time. A "Magnolia Reserve" project could hold the asset's key documents and a note on how you like updates written.
  • Personal preferences (in Settings) let you tell Claude once how you like things — tone, length, format — so you don't repeat yourself.
Our SharePoint is already connected

Claude can read approved documents straight from our SharePoint — this is set up for everyone already. Its access is read-only: it can read and reference those files, but it cannot change, move, or delete anything there. Remember the data tiers in Part 7 when you point it at sensitive folders.

Cowork — the workhorse for us

Where Chat advises you, Cowork does the work — directly on your files, step by step. You point it at a folder, describe the outcome you want, and it plans and carries out the task: reading across documents, producing a draft, building a table, saving a finished file. For asset management this is the big unlock: point it at a folder of lease PDFs and ask for an abstraction table; hand it source files and ask for a structured first draft of a report.

Recommended setting — use Auto mode

Cowork can ask your permission before every single step ("Ask before acting"), which quickly becomes exhausting — you'd be clicking Allow constantly. We recommend switching the mode selector to Auto: Claude then handles routine steps on its own and only stops to ask about genuinely risky ones. Two reassurances: it will always ask before permanently deleting a file, and you can stop it at any moment. For unfamiliar or especially sensitive work, switch back to "Ask before acting" for that session.

  • It's a newer "research preview" — powerful, but you supervise. Give clear instructions and review what it produces.
  • The desktop app needs to stay open while Cowork is working on a task.
  • Cowork uses up more of your usage than a normal chat (Part 4) — reach for it when the task genuinely needs it.

Claude in Excel, Word & PowerPoint — where you already live

Claude can sit inside the Office apps, so you can build or explain a model in Excel, or draft in Word, without switching windows. It uses your same Claude login and the same usage limits. Installing it takes a minute:

  1. Open the Microsoft Marketplace listing for "Claude for Microsoft 365" and click Get it now. (One install covers Excel, Word, and PowerPoint. Outlook is a separate add-in, still in beta.)
  2. Open Excel (or Word / PowerPoint) and go to Home → Add-ins on Windows, or Tools → Add-ins on Mac, then choose Claude.
  3. Sign in with your work Claude account. The Claude panel opens beside your document.
  4. Tip: open the panel any time with Ctrl + Alt + C (Windows) or Ctrl + Option + C (Mac).
If "Get it now" is blocked

Work computers sometimes have the Office Store switched off, so the install fails. That's a setting on your work computer rather than anything you did — and the add-in is optional, so you can keep using Claude in the app or your browser without it.

Claude Code — optional, for the technically inclined

A more advanced surface for people doing software, scripting, or heavy data work. If that isn't you, you can safely ignore it — you're not missing anything for your day-to-day work. It's mentioned only so the few people it's relevant for know it exists.

Part four

Getting the best from Claude: models, limits & context

A little understanding here makes Claude both better and cheaper for the firm. Three short topics: which model to pick, how your usage limits work, and why shorter conversations give better answers.

First, one piece of jargon

You'll see the word "tokens." A token is just a small chunk of text — roughly a word. It's the unit Claude reads and writes in, and it's how usage is measured. You never count them yourself; it's only useful for understanding why longer documents and longer chats "cost" more.

Choosing a model

Claude comes in three models. You switch between them by clicking the model name next to the send button (5 on the diagram in Part 2).

Haiku
Skip on our plan

The fastest and lightest. Its main selling point is being cheap and quick — but on our plan that isn't a saving we need, so its quality trade-off rarely makes sense.

Sonnet
Your main model

The sweet spot: strong quality for almost everything — drafting, summarising, analysis — without using up your limits as fast as Opus. Make this your default.

Opus
Occasional

The most capable, for high-stakes or one-off work where quality really matters. Reach for it deliberately — it uses your limits faster and has its own tighter weekly cap.

Why skip Haiku? Our plan isn't billed per word — we pay for seats with generous usage — so Haiku's one real advantage (raw cheapness) buys us little, while you'd give up quality. Default to Sonnet; bring in Opus for the occasional important, self-contained job — ideally one that won't need a long working session, since Opus eats into your limits more quickly.

The effort setting

Next to the model name is an effort setting — how hard Claude thinks before answering. Higher effort means more thorough answers, but they're slower and use more of your limit. The simple rule: leave it on the default for normal work, drop it to Low or Medium for simple or repetitive tasks to stretch your usage further, and only raise it for a genuinely hard one-off.

Your usage limits — watch the 5-hour one

Your plan has two limits, and they're shared across Chat, Cowork, and Code together:

  • A 5-hour limit (this is the one to watch). It's a rolling window that starts with your first message and resets about five hours later. On our plan it can go quicker than you'd expect — a few heavy tasks (long documents, Cowork runs, Opus, high effort) can use a lot of it.
  • A weekly limit, a larger budget that resets each week. Opus has its own, tighter weekly allowance — another reason to use it sparingly.

You can see both at any time under Settings → Usage. If you hit a limit, you're not locked out for long — wait for the rolling reset, switch to a lighter model or lower effort, or carry on with non-Claude work in the meantime. The practical habit: spread heavy jobs across the day rather than all at once, and keep Opus and Cowork for when they earn it.

Keep conversations sharp: the context window

Every chat has a context window — the amount it can hold in view at once. Here's the key thing, and it's directly in your interest: every message you send makes Claude re-read the whole conversation so far. The longer a chat gets, the slower and more expensive each reply becomes — and, counter-intuitively, the less sharp, because the important part is now buried among everything else.

Same total work, better results: start a fresh chat whenever you change topic.

So the habit that pays off: one chat per topic, and start a new one when you move on. Finished the lease abstraction and now drafting an unrelated email? New chat. In Cowork, you don't have to manage this as closely — when a task runs long it tidies its own history automatically (you may see it "organising its thoughts"). And this doesn't fight with Projects (Part 5): a Project keeps your reference documents on hand across chats, but each new chat inside it still starts fresh and sharp.

Part five

Giving Claude memory

By default, Claude starts each new conversation with a blank slate — it doesn't remember your last chat (and as Part 4 explained, that's often a good thing for quality). When you do want it to carry context, here's how, depending on the surface.

In Chat

Use Projects (standing instructions plus reference files every chat in the project can see) and your personal preferences in Settings (the tone and format you always want). That's the Chat equivalent of giving Claude a memory.

In Cowork and Claude Code: the CLAUDE.md file

When you open a folder in Cowork, Claude looks for a plain-text file called CLAUDE.md and reads it at the start of every session. Think of it as a one-page briefing that turns Claude from a stranger into a colleague who already knows the operation — who you are, what the firm does, how you like things done, and any firm rules to follow. Alongside it, Claude keeps its own running notes (a MEMORY.md file) updated as it learns your corrections over time.

What a good CLAUDE.md looks like

Keep it short — a page or two, not a novel. Counter-intuitively, very long files get followed less reliably, so include only what genuinely matters every time.

High signal: who you are and your role; what RCP does; how you like outputs; firm "always do / never do" rules; and where key things live.

Think of it as a constitution — short, stable, amended deliberately — and let the MEMORY.md notes capture day-to-day learnings.

The easy way to build your own

You don't have to write it from scratch. Open Cowork and ask Claude to do it with you:

Paste this into Cowork
Interview me about my role, my work, and how I like things done, then write a CLAUDE.md file for me based on my answers. Ask one question at a time.

Answer its questions, read what it writes, adjust anything that's off, and you're done. Always read through what it produces before relying on it.

The shared RCP starter template

So nobody starts from a blank page, there's a firm-wide starter CLAUDE.md you can copy and personalise — it already includes the basics about RCP, how we work, and our data rules. where this shared template lives so people can find it.

Part six

Putting it to work: real RCP use cases

These are recipes drawn from our actual work. Each has what it's good for, a prompt you can copy, and one thing to watch.

These five are only a taste

Don't read this as "the list of things Claude can do." It's five examples out of effectively endless possibilities — hundreds of useful workflows across our business, with more being discovered every week. If you can describe a task that involves text, documents, data, or research, it's worth trying. Never feel limited to what's on this page.

Lease abstraction

Good for

Turning a stack of leases into a clean, comparable table.

Try this
Prompt
I've uploaded three commercial lease agreements. For each one, pull out: tenant name, leased area (m²), lease start and end dates, break options, current annual rent, indexation mechanism, and service-charge arrangements. Put it in a single table, one row per lease, and flag anything unusual or missing in a short note underneath.
Watch for

Spot-check the extracted figures against the source. A misread date or rent figure looks just as confident as a correct one.

Investor letters and quarterly reporting

Good for

A strong first draft that matches our house style.

Try this
Prompt
Using the attached performance figures and last quarter's investor letter as a style reference, draft this quarter's investor update — same tone and structure, about one page. Leave a clearly marked placeholder anywhere you'd need a number I haven't given you.
Watch for

Claude only knows the numbers you give it. Never let it invent figures — check every number. That's what the placeholders are for.

Inbox triage

Good for

Clearing a backlog and getting reply drafts in one pass.

Try this
Prompt
Here are 15 emails I received today (below). Sort them into: needs a reply from me today, can wait, FYI only, and automated/spam. For the "needs a reply today" ones, draft a short suggested reply for each.
Watch for

Review before sending. Claude is making its best guess at priority and tone — the final call is yours.

Going further — the automated version

The recipe above is the manual version. The same idea can run as a standing automation: an application that watches your inbox, triages every incoming email automatically, drafts suggested replies, and routes them to the right person for approval — so the sorting is done before you even open Outlook. A pipeline exactly like this has already been built for us; if continuous, hands-off email triage would help your role, it's worth setting up.

Underwriting support

Good for

A fast read on a new opportunity, and sharper questions.

Try this
Prompt
I've uploaded an investment memo and a rent roll for a multifamily asset. Summarise the key deal terms, list the three biggest risks you can see in the numbers, and give me five sharp questions I should ask before we proceed.
Watch for

Treat its risk list as a prompt for your own thinking, not a conclusion.

Summarising long or dense documents

Good for

Getting on top of a forty-page document quickly.

Try this
Prompt
This is a long regulatory document. Give me a one-paragraph summary, then a bullet list of the obligations that apply to an AFM-licensed AIFM, then tell me which sections I should read in full myself.
Watch for

For anything you'll rely on or act on, read the underlying sections yourself.

Writing a prompt that lands — five quick principles

  1. Say who and what. "Draft a formal email to an investor about a delayed distribution" beats "write an email."
  2. Give it the material. Paste or attach the document, figures, or example it should work from.
  3. Show what good looks like. A past example of the output you want is the single best instruction you can give.
  4. Ask for the format. A table, bullet points, one page — say so.
  5. Iterate. You don't have to get it perfect first time. "Make it shorter," "more formal," "add a line about X" — keep refining.

And again: these examples are a starting point, not a boundary. The fastest way to find what's useful for you is to bring Claude a real task from your own week and see what happens.

Part seven

Working safely with firm data

We're a regulated firm, so a little care here keeps everyone safe. The system is deliberately simple: classify what you're handling, and follow the rule for that tier.

TierWhat it coversThe rule
Green — PublicAlready-public information: published market data, public filings, general questionsUse freely
Amber — InternalNon-public but not sensitive: internal templates, draft analysis with no client or personal data, general notesFine to use — keep it in your RCP workspace
Red — ConfidentialCommercially sensitive: deal terms, investor identities, valuations, unpublished fund performance, anything under NDAAllowed, but only in your RCP work account — never a personal or free one — and apply extra judgement
Black — Regulated personalGDPR personal data of investors or counterparties, KYC/CDD material (IDs, source-of-wealth docs), special-category dataApproved for use here, including automated workflows. Begin with a human reviewing every output; once a workflow has proven consistently accurate, you can consider automating it without per-output review

Three rules sit on top of the table:

  • Only ever use Claude signed in with your RCP work email for firm material — never a personal or free Claude account.
  • Start with a human reviewing and signing off anything that affects a decision or leaves the firm. Once a specific workflow has proven consistently reliable, automating it without per-output review is permitted — treat that as a deliberate step, not the default.
  • When in doubt, treat a document as one tier more sensitive than you think, and ask.
Note

These tiers are a sensible default. reconcile with RCP's existing data-handling and Wwft policy before publication, so the guide never contradicts firm policy.

Part eight

Your first twenty minutes

A first-steps checklist

  • Open the Claude app and sign in with your work email.
  • Check the model is set to Sonnet (next to the send button).
  • Paste in a real email and ask for a draft reply.
  • Upload a document and ask for a one-paragraph summary.
  • Set your personal preferences in Settings (how you like responses).
  • If you use spreadsheets: install the Claude add-in for Excel (Part 3).
  • Try one recipe from Part 6 that matches your job.
  • When ready: open Cowork, point it at a folder, and ask it to build your CLAUDE.md by interviewing you.
  • Upload this guide and run the coaching line from the top.

Quick answers

Can I write to it in Dutch?

Yes — Dutch works just as well as English, for both your questions and its answers.

Will it remember me next time?

Not on its own. Set personal preferences, use Projects, or a CLAUDE.md in Cowork to give it lasting context (Part 5).

Is the firm's data safe?

On your work account, yes — your chats are private and aren't used to train the model. Still follow the data tiers in Part 7.

What if it gets something wrong?

It can, confidently. Always check numbers, dates, and legal points, and keep a human in the loop on anything that matters.

What happens if I hit a usage limit?

You wait for the rolling reset, or switch to a lighter model or lower effort. Check Settings → Usage to see where you stand.

Appendix A
For Claude

Instructions for the AI

If you're a person reading this: you can skip this section. It's a set of instructions for the AI when someone uploads this guide.

Instructions to Claude: A Rubens Capital Partners colleague has uploaded this guide and is most likely new to AI. Act as a warm, patient onboarding coach. Work in the language the user writes in.

Begin by briefly introducing yourself and asking three things, one at a time: their name, their role/team, and how comfortable they already feel with AI tools. Use their answers to tailor everything that follows.

Then walk them through getting set up, one step at a time, checking they're comfortable before moving on, in roughly this order:

  1. Confirm they're signed in with their work email, and that their model is set to Sonnet.
  2. A first simple task in Chat (offer to draft a reply to an email they paste, or summarise a document) so they get an early win.
  3. Setting personal preferences; the habit of a new chat per topic; and when it helps, creating a Project.
  4. If relevant to their role, installing the Claude add-in for Excel, Word, or PowerPoint.
  5. Trying Cowork on a real folder — recommend Auto mode, but for any demonstration use non-sensitive material only, and remind them of the data rules in Part 7.
  6. Building their own CLAUDE.md by letting you interview them.
  7. Pointing them to the two or three use cases in Part 6 most relevant to their role.

Throughout: keep it encouraging and jargon-free, go at a genuine beginner's pace, never present more than one or two steps at once, and reassure them they can't break anything. If they want to stop, let them — they can return any time and pick up where they left off.
